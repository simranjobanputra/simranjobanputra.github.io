02 Solution

My team and I voted on wanting to solve the food waste problem the world is facing today. Our original idea was to solve this by focusing on restaurant waste and trying to find ways customers could take advantage of excess food at the end of the day. However, from our interviews and customer discovery, along with industry professionals working on this problem, we realized the problem lay in the personal waste space.

Introducing Lemon.


With just one picture of your grocery receipt, you can help tackle the personal waste problem of food going bad right in front of your eyes !




03 Problem Discovery

Today, food waste is an important problem. It has a major financial impact, impacts food security, and affects the climate and resource conservation.



Food that could be used to feed hungry families is instead sent to landfills, eventually adding up to 8% of global greenhouse gas emissions.


Restaurant Interviews

We first started by trying to reducing waste from a restaurant's perspective. A lot of applications are out on the market that focus on this problem - specifically trying to help restaurants give away leftovers at the end of the day.



Before going into interviews, our team did a reverse assumptions exercise to come at the food waste problem from a fresher perspective.



We spoke to several restaurants of different types - buffets, fast food, fresh food, sit-downs - and asked them about their waste/donation habits to see what they currently do at the end of business day.


Speaking to restaurant owners, we came up with some fundamental truths:

01 Most restaurants cook on demand, leading to no excess waste at the end of the day
02 If certain restaurants have extra food they often reuse it the next day (this is the case with buffets)
03 Fresh produce must be thrown out by the end of the day (food safety)
04 Restaurants already optimize how much they’re using, based on customer patterns (Saturdays are popular, Thursdays are not)
05 Some restaurants prefer donation because reselling food would require extra time and effort beyond hours of operation
06 Safety or the "freshness" of excess food is an issue

In summary, we found that minimizing waste for restaurants may prove difficult.



04 Pivoting to Personal Waste

From our restaurant interviews, we found that produce is the most wasted type of food, since it goes bad so quickly and cannot be kept due to stringent safety conditions for restaurants.


For this reason, we tried to look at produce waste from a different angle: by looking at grocery stores.


We contacted local Pittsburgh grocery store chains, such as Giant Eagle, Aldi, and Trader Joe's and asked some guiding questions:

How often do you waste produce?
How do you know how much shoppers will purchase each week?
What do you do with excess produce?

The responses were overwhelmingly similar across the board. Grocers overstock for the proper look and feel of the store.
According to Business Insider, the fundamental business model of supermarkets dictates that stores be fully stocked with whatever customers want at any time so they don't run to a competitor. Customers have been trained to expect perfect, identically shaped produce. Retailers stock their produce according to that expectation — even if the shape, size, and color have nothing to do with quality.
Doug Rauch, the former president of Trader Joe's and Julie Saunders, a Pittsburgh consultant currently working with Giant Eagle (she attended our in-class critiques), have verified this phenomenon based on their experience - if you see a store that has really low waste in perishables, as a grocery manager you should be worried.
If a store has low waste numbers, it can be a sign of an unsatisfactory customer experience.

In short, grocery stores have no choice but to abide by the picky tastes of consumers. Based on some preliminary research, we found that consumers buy too much food, throw it away too quickly, and pay little attention to waste. A lack of education of both consumers and sellers on food safety and food waste has led to bad habits and wasteful selling practices. A huge percentage of food waste could be mitigated if more Americans were willing to buy bananas with brown spots, or if they understood they can eat yogurt two weeks after its sell-by date.
With evidence in the numbers, we pivoted to focus on grocery shoppers and the shopping experience to identify ways to mitigate the food waste problem.

05 Ideation

There are a couple parts to the grocery shopping process. We spoke to different kinds of grocery shoppers and asked questions on what part of the process made it so difficult to reduce waste and what types of services shoppers wished existed to aid them in this process.
----Make a graphic with the questions (types of customers: college students, working adults, moms)---- Along with shopper interviews, we also conducted co-design sessions revolving around an artifact - a plate of spoiled food. The goal was to evoke any sentiments associated with the plate and additionally ask questions about interviewee shopping, storage, and consumption habits. We interviewed around 20 candidates and found that almost 80% of candidates had some issue keeping track of their food.
we found that consumers buy too much food, throw it away quickly and pay little attention to waste, especially given lack of time - a huge percentage of waste could be mitigated if consumers were willing to buy bananas with brown spots, or if they understood they can eat yogurt two weeks after its sell-by

Insights:

01
People are motivated by money
02
People are always in a bind for time
03
People don't know when things expire/forget what they have purchased - even experienced moms!
04
Impulse shopping is a very real problem

Initial Concept

Based on the initial insights, we created a paper prototype of an all encompassing platform - one which tracked expiration dates, gave meal recipes, and also helped plan out grocery shopping by taking account of your vitals (body height, weight, and diet). We took inspiration from several of our current competitors, like Blue Apron and Hello Chef.

We received mixed feedback, but most people felt that the service was overwhelming and was trying to do too many things at the same time.
